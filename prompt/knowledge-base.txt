Field-proven questions — surfaced across 13 live calls
These questions emerged from analyzing real demo calls. Each one consistently unlocked better discovery than standard funnel questions. Use them across all verticals before going into the problem-area sequences above.
Funnel leak locator — best discovery question in the dataset
"Where do you feel like the biggest disconnect is — from ad to booking, from booking to showing up, or from showing up to converting?"
Forces the prospect to self-identify exactly where they're leaking rather than just describing their traffic sources. The answer tells you which Ageless capability to lead with. A prospect who says "ad to booking" needs the visualization and lead quality story. One who says "showing up to converting" needs the pre-sold patient and consultation efficiency angle. One who says "booking to showing up" has a commitment problem — the Future Aging Self is your entry point.
Practice structure — ask this early, it changes everything
"Is it just you at the practice, or do you have staff handling inbound and follow-up?"
Solo vs. staffed is a fundamental fork in how you pitch. A solo operator can't run a disciplined lead follow-up cadence — for them, automation is the entire value proposition, not a nice-to-have. A staffed practice has a delegation problem. Ask this in the first three minutes. Learning it at minute 21 means you've been pitching the wrong angle the whole call.
Capacity and urgency check
"Are you at a place where you're really looking to fill the schedule, or are you pretty full right now?"
Reveals urgency, growth stage, and which use case to lead with. Open capacity → acquisition story. Full schedule → cross-sell and reactivation. Also surfaces new provider or new location situations where there's sudden capacity and genuine urgency.
Failed marketing excavation — don't skip it
"You mentioned you've tried marketing before — what happened when you followed up with those leads? What was the response like?"
When a prospect says marketing hasn't worked, most reps move on. Don't. The failure story is the setup for Ageless's entire positioning. Dig in: What kind of ads? What was the call-to-action? What did people say when you called? The answer almost always reveals promotion-anchored traffic that brought price-shoppers — which sets up the curiosity-anchored vs. discount-anchored reframe perfectly.
Decision-maker check — confirm before investing 40 minutes
"Will the person who makes the final call on something like this be on today, or will you be presenting this to someone afterward?"
Two of six calls in the dataset had the decision-maker not in the room. In both cases the deal went to a follow-up without the rep present. Ask this at the very start. If the decision-maker isn't on, restructure toward "here's what you need to present" and make sure your post-call email goes directly to the decision-maker, not just through the proxy.
For existing RepeatMD customers — ask this before anything else
"How are you currently using RepeatMD and what's working well for you?"
Six calls in the dataset involved current or paused RepeatMD customers — Matt, Stephanie, Joyce, Jamey, Elliot, Dwayne — and a clear pattern has emerged. Every single one was either underutilizing the platform, paused, or actively frustrated with some aspect of RMD. Joyce's practice was only using Affirm financing. Stephanie had a months-long Canadian tax billing problem. Dwayne's account was paused during a build-out. Elliot's was paused entirely. Matt discovered Ageless through the admin portal and only used RMD lightly.
Asking this question early does three things: (1) it surfaces the underutilization problem so you can give them a quick activation win (staff language, QR code placement, gift card promos) that builds trust before you pitch anything new; (2) it sets up Ageless as the thing that makes the investment they're already making worthwhile — "you've got the retention engine, Ageless is what fills it with the right patients in the first place"; (3) it flags any open RMD issues that need to be routed to CS before you can close — trying to upsell on top of a live billing complaint reads as tone-deaf and kills the deal.
For RMD-paused prospects specifically:
follow up with "What would need to be true for you to unpause RepeatMD alongside this?" This gives you the account's actual needs and flags whether they're a candidate for the combined retention + acquisition play once Ageless proves out. Don't skip this on any existing or former RMD customer call. The churn risk you defuse becomes the trust that closes the upsell.

Continuation
Second calls, multi-touch deals, and the work between conversations — where most Ageless deals are actually won or lost
Ageless sits in a category with more decision-makers than most vertical SaaS. A medspa, plastic surgery, or wellness practice buying a patient acquisition tool often involves a marketing agency, a partner physician, a spouse, or an office manager — any of whom may or may not be in the room on the first call.
When the room is complete and the decision-maker is present, close in the room.
When the buying committee extends beyond the call, the second conversation is where the deal is actually worked — and running it with the same intention as the first is what separates reps who carry those deals to close from reps who lose them to silence. This section is the playbook for the multi-stakeholder version of the deal.
🧭

Verbal close with a confirmed follow-up time, named attendees, and a clear activation path. The deal is won — the second call is implementation, not selling.

The move:
At the end of every first call, label the deal in one of these three states before you close the laptop. If you can't, you didn't close the first call tightly enough — and the second call will inherit that ambiguity.

Running the Second Call
Three frameworks that reliably advance a deal — not just continue it
The Re-Open — set the outcome before they do
Don't open with "great to see you again." Open with the ask.
"I want to make sure this call is worth both your time and [new person]'s. What's the outcome that would make this a productive 30 minutes for you?"
This is a lock-the-call move at the start instead of at the end. It works better because the prospect hasn't yet spent political capital saying "I'm not buying." You're getting their definition of success on the record before the conversation starts — which means you now know what you're aiming at, and they've committed to it out loud.
Read the answer:
If the new person answers — you know who's really running the decision.
If your original champion answers — the new person is along for the ride, which is a different sale.
If nobody answers clearly — you're not going to close this call and you should adjust expectations immediately.
The Translation Gap — recover the pitch your champion mangled
Between call one and call two, your champion has had a conversation about you without you. They translated your pitch — usually badly.
"What did you tell [partner/marketing person] about this after our first call? I want to make sure I reinforce what resonated and clean up anything that didn't land."
This surfaces the translation gap instantly. You'll almost always hear a version of your pitch that's missing the one thing that would have closed it. Your job in the next ten minutes is to put that thing back in the room, in front of the person who didn't hear it the first time.
Follow-up if the answer is thin:
"What questions did they have that you couldn't fully answer because I wasn't there?" That's the objection you're handling on this call, whether it gets surfaced otherwise or not.
The Proxy Interview — when the DM still isn't in the room
If you run a second call and the decision-maker isn't there, stop pitching. Extract.
Ask these in sequence, pausing on each. You're using the gatekeeper as a research tool, not trying to close them.
"If [DM name] were on this call right now, what's the first question they'd ask?"
"What are they worried about that you think they wouldn't say out loud?"
"What would make them say yes on the spot versus want to think about it?"
"What's the last tool like this they said no to, and why?"
End the call with the ask:
"Based on what you just told me, I'd like fifteen minutes directly with [DM]. Can you set that up this week?" That's the close. Not another group meeting — a direct line.
Why this works:
The gatekeeper can't close. Treating them like they can is how Skin Serenity-type deals stall indefinitely. Use them as a source of intelligence, then escalate to the person who can sign.

Between Calls
The texts, emails, and touchpoints that keep a deal warm — and when to send them
Pre-Meeting Ammunition — the email your champion actually needs
Most reps send a recap after the first call. That's defense. Offense is arming the champion
before
their internal meeting.
Send a second email
24 hours before
your champion's internal conversation — not 24 hours after your demo. Structure it this way:
Name the meeting explicitly: "You're meeting with [marketing person / partner / spouse] on [day]."
List the three questions the absent stakeholder is most likely to ask, with your answers to each (method below).
Calibrate those three questions to the gatekeeper archetype — a marketing agency's questions are not a partner physician's questions.
Close with: "If any of this doesn't sound right or you'd rather have me on the call, I can join with 15 minutes of notice."
That last sentence is the move. It gives your champion permission to invite you into the meeting without losing face. Roughly one in three will take you up on it. The ones who don't are now better armed than they would have been.

The three questions aren't a guess. They come from the first call transcript plus what you know about the gatekeeper archetype. Reading the Room
When to close in the room, when to set up the second act — and why the distinction matters
The mindset shift
Same-day close is always the goal when the full buying committee is in the room. The nuance in this category is that the committee isn't always complete on call one — a medspa, plastic surgery, or wellness practice often has two to four stakeholders involved in a tool decision, and some of them are reached through your champion rather than directly. When that's the shape of the deal, forcing a same-day close on an incomplete room is how you break the relationship with your champion — they feel pressured, go quiet, and you lose the deal you would have won with a better second call.
When the room is complete, close it. When it isn't, run the second call as a main event — not a recovery move.
Reps who internalize this read the room more accurately on call one. They close the deals that are closeable, and they set up the multi-stakeholder deals to win on call two — instead of spending their energy fighting for a commitment the buyer can't legitimately make yet.

Gap analysis
The thing that was supposed to close the deal is what killed it
On the first call, the single highest-value unplayed asset was Linda's colleague Ella — a peer who had just gone live with Ageless that week. The recommendation in the first-call analysis was explicit: reach Ella in the WhatsApp community before Friday, make a direct introduction, and let Ella's experience close Linda. Instead, Linda talked to Ella directly and unmediated — and learned that Ella was on a free trial. That fact alone reframed Linda's entire read on the $499 founders offer. A price that felt fair on Friday felt punitive on Monday.
Second compounding factor: Linda used the two-day gap to think more carefully about her own economics. She surfaced on this call that luminaMD skincare sales in her clinic have dropped sharply year-over-year — patients cutting discretionary spend down to cleanser + vitamin C only. In a contracting spend environment, a $500/month bet on a new acquisition tool reads differently than it did during the emotional peak of the first-call demo.
What worked on the second call
Strong
Dominique didn't argue the Ella situation — acknowledged it immediately
When Linda raised the free trial issue, Dominique explained the timing cleanly: the trial was a launch-period offer that expired, it wasn't even available at AmSpa, lots of practices were hearing about it retroactively through peers. No defensiveness, no pushback on whether Ella should have mentioned it. This was the right posture — disputing Linda's read would have damaged the relationship; validating it preserved the option of a future deal.
When a prospect surfaces information that undermines your pricing positioning, acknowledge the underlying fairness of their read before you re-frame. Dominique did this well. The rep move that fails in this situation is defending the current price aggressively — that converts a "no for now" into a "never."
Strong
Framed the tier price as "proof-of-itself" rather than a standard monthly subscription
Dominique's strongest moment on the call was reframing the $499 not as a price but as a commitment-test structure: "the tiers could be a little bit on the pricier side, but the difference between our tiers and working with an agency or consultant is it's really designed to prove itself to you — unlimited first month, no contract, walk away if it's not working." This is the right frame for an operator who has been burned by marketing spend before, which Linda has been (three failed agency attempts from the first-call analysis).
The "prove itself" framing is currently scattered across various pricing talk tracks and should be formalized. It's specifically effective against the "$499 is too much" objection because it reframes the purchase as a test, not a recurring cost. Worth a named entry in the Objections tab.
What didn't work — and where the deal actually died
Gap
The Toronto economic context landed unanswered
Linda's clearest reason for passing wasn't the price or the Ella situation — it was the macroeconomic read she articulated at 3:13: "patients are not spending as they were, we're seeing a very big decline in what patients are spending, living costs in Ontario is quite high." This is a real business condition, not a stall tactic. Dominique's response didn't engage with it — she pivoted to the relationship play ("if there ever is any promotion I'll keep you top of mind"). That's a fine tactical close, but it leaves the strategic objection unanswered.
The answer that would have re-opened the door: "If your patients are spending less on luxury skincare, they're not spending less on looking younger — that's a wellness investment, not a discretionary one. Ageless is the tool designed for exactly this market: helping you reach patients who still want the outcome when they've cut the add-ons." That reframes contraction as the problem Ageless is built to solve, not the reason to skip it.
The "economic contraction" objection is going to recur — Canada and the US are both in the same softening spend environment. A standard talk track for this objection should be built into the Objections tab. The core move: the tightening market is the argument *for* a conversion-focused tool, not against. Every dollar spent on unqualified traffic is wasted; Ageless makes each dollar work harder, which matters more in a down market, not less.
Gap
Didn't use the Continuation playbook — no re-open question, no translation-gap surface
The Continuation tab prescribes opening second calls with the question "what's the outcome that would make this a productive conversation?" Dominique opened instead with "do you have any questions you want me to run through with you again?" — a passive opener that let Linda control the frame from word one. Given Linda had already talked to Ella and made her decision, she was going to pass regardless, but the passive opener made the "no" arrive faster than a more structured re-engagement would have.
The translation-gap question — "what did you tell Ella about this after our first call?" — would also have surfaced the free-trial landmine earlier in the conversation, giving Dominique a chance to address it before Linda delivered her decision.
This is the first real test of the Continuation playbook in the dataset and it wasn't used. Worth making it the standard second-call template for Dominique and the team — opening with the outcome question and the translation-gap question, in that order, before anything else.
The broader signal — this is a win, not a loss
Relationship
Linda called specifically to decline — that's a signal, not a setback
Most prospects who decide not to buy simply go quiet. Linda didn't. She took the follow-up call specifically to tell Dominique "no" in person. Her words: "I did want to take your call and tell you that personally." That's not a prospect who's done — that's a prospect who values the relationship and is keeping the door open. The final exchange confirmed it: she asked Dominique to save her number and stay in touch about future promotions.
Three realistic paths back into this account: (1) a future founders-period discount or equivalent; (2) Ella's results, if they end up strong, become the reference Linda needed; (3) the Ontario economic softness eases. Dominique's posture preserved all three.
Lessons from the call — not a scorecard (yet)
The Continuation playbook didn't exist when this call was run. In fact, this call was part of what inspired the tab. So this isn't a scorecard against Dominique — it's a retrospective on what the tape taught us that's now baked into the framework every rep has access to going forward.
What the tape taught us about peer references:
Unmediated peer conversations can surface information that reshapes the entire deal read (Ella's free trial). The lesson — now in the playbook — is to make the introduction yourself, in the community, with context, before the prospect reaches out cold.
What the tape taught us about second-call openers:
Starting with "do you have any questions?" lets the prospect control the frame. The outcome-anchor open ("what would make this a productive conversation?") is now the standard because of calls like this one.
What the tape taught us about translation gaps:
The Ella free-trial landmine would have surfaced earlier with a translation-gap question ("what did you tell Ella about this after our first call?"). That question is now named in the playbook specifically because this deal showed its value.
What Dominique did right under real conditions:
Didn't argue the Ella situation, didn't defend the price aggressively, delivered the "prove itself" reframe cleanly, preserved the relationship for future re-engagement. On a cold second call with a decided prospect, those moves matter more than scripted plays.
The honest read:
Linda's decision was probably already made by the time this call started. The deal died when she talked to Ella unmediated, not during this four-minute conversation. The value of this tape isn't in what should have been done differently — it's that it showed us what a second-call framework needed to include. The Continuation tab exists partly because of it.
